Background

Growing up in LA, music was all around me. From high school cellist to self-taught bedroom producer and GameBoy DJ, I loved exploring sound in any way I could. So I’ve been a fan of Dublab, an independent online radio station started in 1999, for a very long time.

Dublab had grown — but its identity was static. It wasn’t communicating their core ethos: a raw, experimental platform for the growth of music, art, culture, and everything in between.

Drawing with Sound

Most of Dublab’s audience are familiar with digital music tools, many of which can be used for visualizing sound. Dublab’s identity takes it a step further: drawing with sound.

The code is based on Ted Davis’s XYscope library for Processing, modified for use with both static images and a Kinect camera or webcam. It is used for both static visuals and a large-scale, interactive sound installation. Without getting overly technical, it works like a feedback loop: images are translated to sound, sound is visualized on the oscilloscope, then that sound is filtered, which affects the image again. But you don’t need to know all that to use it. Basically, it’s just really fun to play with.


DB Circuit Mono

Circuit is a display typeface combining the idea of a circuit and a musical staff. The grid is based on a musical staff: 5 lines with one interval between each. The method of drawing is based on the concept of an electrical circuit and the way a vector display (i.e., oscilloscope) renders type: as continuous, connected lines. The typeface is built in Glyphs with two styles that can be layered to create the connected effect.

DB Data Mono

Taking inspiration from the typographic language of early synthesizers and sci-fi cinema, Data Mono is a re-drawing of Data70 as a true monospaced font. To relate to the idea of sound, there are two variatiants: the original, analog, “sine wave” variant, and hard-edged, digital, “square wave” variant built on the same anatomy.
