What It Is

Dublab is an online radio station based in Los Angeles.

Why It Matters

Growing up in LA, music was all around me. From high school cellist to self-taught bedroom producer and GameBoy DJ, I loved exploring sound in any way I could. So I’ve been a fan of Dublab, an independent online radio station started in 1999, for a very long time. Dublab had grown — but its identity was static. It wasn’t communicating their core ethos: a raw, experimental platform for the growth of music, art, culture, and everything in between.


Drawing with Sound

Most of Dublab’s audience are familiar with digital music tools, many of which can be used for visualizing sound. Dublab’s identity takes it a step further: drawing with sound.

Creating Tools

The code is based on Ted Davis’s XYscope library for Processing, modified for use with both static images and a Kinect camera or webcam. It is used for both static visuals and a large-scale, interactive sound installation. It works like a feedback loop: images are translated to sound, sound is visualized on the oscilloscope, then that sound is filtered, which affects the image again.


The Processing sketch used to generate poster linework.
A phone running TouchOSC controls the filter value for the sound image.

Custom Typefaces

Two typefaces were developed for Dublab based around sound: DB Data Mono is a true monospace revival of Data70 in sine- and square wave variants; DB Circuit Mono is a connected digital “script” based on the way type is rendered on oscilloscopes.

In Conversation

Talking to Mark, he became a partner in the process. I took the initiative to talk to the client directly and asked, what would be useful to you? He mentioned that Dublab had a large archive of interviews with musicians that he felt was important to preserve. I proposed a print version. I used the same text tool I built for Indeterminacy to create chapter breaks, and DB Circuit Mono for the cover typography. The first edition is a conversation with Van Dyke
